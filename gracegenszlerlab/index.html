The Physics of Gymnastics


I grew up doing gymnastics and started competing as a level 4 in fifth grade. By the time I reached eigth grade, I was a level 7. Once I started high school, I joined the varsity team. After graduating high school, I took a break from gymnastics for seven years during undergrad, my gap year, and first 1.5 years of grad school. In 2022 I joined the Cornell Club Gymnastics Team and started competing again through the National Association of Intercollegiate Gymnastics Clubs.

Gymnastics is part of why I got into physics in the first place. One of the biggest obstacles to learning a new skill is fear. You're flipping around in the air and twisting adds another level of complexity. The high bar is high. Splitting the beam hurts. A lot. The vault table is just sitting there and you have to run at it. But, what I found during high school is that if I could reduce a skill down to the physics fundamentals to understand how it worked, then things became less scary.

I've since graduated Cornell, but have no plans to stop doing gymnastics. I'm many years older now, but also four physics and engineering degrees smarter. So, I'm putting my degrees to use to analyze the physics of gymnastics. I want to see how many skills I can get back and if I can learn new ones.
