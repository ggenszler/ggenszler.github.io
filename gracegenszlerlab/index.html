The Physics of Gymnastics

Check out my YouTube to watch my gymnastics progress and learn about physics along the way! New videos posted on Sundays.

I grew up doing gymnastics and started competing as a level 4 in fifth grade. By the time I reached eighth grade, I was a level 7. Once I started high school, I joined the varsity team. After graduating high school, I took a break from gymnastics for seven years during undergrad, my gap year, and the first year and a half of grad school.

In 2022, I joined the Cornell Club Gymnastics Team and started competing again through the National Association of Intercollegiate Gymnastics Clubs.

Gymnastics is part of why I got into physics in the first place. One of the biggest obstacles to learning a new skill is fear. You're flipping around in the air, and twisting adds another level of complexity. The high bar is high. Splitting the beam hurts — a lot. The vault table is just sitting there, and you have to run directly at it.

What I found during high school, though, was that if I could reduce a skill down to the underlying physics fundamentals and understand how it worked, things became much less scary.

I've since graduated Cornell, but I have no plans to stop doing gymnastics. I'm many years older now, but also four physics and engineering degrees smarter. So, I'm putting those degrees to use to analyze the physics of gymnastics, see how many skills I can get back, and determine whether I can learn entirely new ones.
