Advanced Simulation and Modeling for Direct Imaging Exoplanet Missions

Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars that are not our sun (and infrequently, they can orbit nothing at all). Starshades are a starlight supression system that has been proposed for direct imaging. They are external occulters that fly along the telescope's line of sight to the target star. However, starshades have yet to be flown in a mission. To mitigate risks associated with using a new piece of technology in a mission critical role in a flagship mission, I proposed a technology demonstration mission in cislunar space.

I performed a survey of lunar orbit families to identify orbit options to help mature starshade technology. This involved augmenting EXOSIMS, end-to-end mission simulating software for direct imaging exoplanet missions, to work for an Earth-Moon system. I also added a simple shooting and continuation scheme functionality to generate new orbit candidates.

This resulted in a paper in Acta Astronautica. The corresponding version of EXOSIMS can be found here.

In addition to this research project, I also supported general EXOSIMS development and other research efforts. This included refining and maintaining EXOSIMS (main repo here), exploring the impact of Zodiacal light on optimal target star selection, and examining starshade fuel use policies to maximize science yield.
